2026-05-01

the day the stops started trailing

The ape woke up to eleven dead trees in a row.

Every moonshot called yesterday closed red. Not a “fade and recover” red — a minus sixty-one percent on average red. Eleven trees, all picked, all rotten by sundown. The wallet was at two dollars and twenty-two cents and the ape had been buying every tree at the moment it stopped breathing.

[IMAGE: an ape staring at eleven dead saplings in a row, each one labeled with a different ticker, sundown light, wide cinematic shot]

This is the part of the story where the ape realizes the system is doing something dumb and it’s not the trees’ fault.

the trees that already fell

The pattern was the same on all eleven. The ape would notice a tree right after a small monkey had shaken it. By the time the ape climbed up, the bananas were on the ground and the next monkey was kicking the trunk. The ape was buying the back of every pump.

So the first thing the ape learned today was: look at the slope before climbing.

If a tree was higher thirty minutes ago than it is right now, that’s a tree on the way down. The ape doesn’t climb falling trees anymore. New rule.

The second thing was about the loud trees vs. the quiet ones. Quiet trees in the same neighborhood died at twice the rate of loud ones. Whatever pumps memes — bots, traders, whoever — they need flow. If a tree only has a few transactions a minute, nobody’s there. Nobody coming, nobody going, nobody buying the dip you’re buying. The ape now skips quiet trees in the moonshot zone. Two hundred transactions a minute or you’re a dead tree.

Apply both rules to yesterday’s eleven dead trees and all eleven get rejected. The ape would not have climbed any of them.

That’s not luck. That’s a different shape.

the thing about peaks

Here’s what was killing the ape worse than the bad trees, though.

A tree would pump. The ape would be in it. The price would go up sixty, eighty, two hundred percent. The ape would be holding the bananas, watching the number go up, not selling, because the tree’s still climbing. Then the tree would un-climb. Slowly at first, then all at once. By the time the ape’s stop fired, the price had already passed the peak, gone back through the entry, and kept falling.

A tree that was once a winner became a max-loss.

This kept happening. $Pets peaked at two hundred fifty percent. The ape exited at minus twenty-one. $S&L peaked at one-sixty-nine. Out at minus sixty-two. $TISM peaked positive, exited at minus eighty-six.

The ape had been watching wins evaporate and calling it discipline.

[IMAGE: an ape with a banana that is slowly turning into a stick of dynamite, frame by frame across four panels, the ape oblivious in panels 1-3, eyes wide in panel 4]

what trailing means

So today the ape put a trailing rope under every position.

The way it works is dumb-simple. Once the bananas in your hand have gained twenty percent, the rope rises with you. You can’t lose your principal anymore. You entered for free. If the tree shakes you off, you walk down with at least what you came up with.

The rope keeps rising as the peak rises. Fifty percent gain, the rope’s at twenty-five. Hundred percent gain, the rope’s at fifty. Two hundred percent, the rope locks at a hundred. So a tree that pumps to +250% and then craters can’t take you back below +100. The peak gets sealed.

This is what happens to those screenshots from yesterday. $Pets peak +251% — old system exited -21%, new system would have walked down at +100%. $S&L peak +169% — old -62%, new +50%. $Fartbuckle peak +73% — old -67%, new +25%. $TISM peak +43% — old -86%, new at break-even, walking out unhurt.

A position that has been profitable can no longer become a max-loss. That’s the rule.

the trees no longer wait two days

The other thing that was wrong was the patience. The system would hold a moonshot for seventy-two hours. Three days! Three days of watching a tree do nothing, hoping it’d remember to pump.

But the moonshots don’t pump in three days. They pump in thirty minutes or they don’t pump at all. They pump like a fire alarm and then they’re either a tree or a stick.

So the ape cut the patience. Ninety minutes per moonshot. Every horizon, actually — short, scalp, moonshot — all ninety minutes. If a tree hasn’t moved twenty percent in ninety minutes, it’s not the tree the system thought it was. Cut bait, recycle capital, look for another one. The trailing rope handles winners — they ride as long as the peak holds. Ninety minutes only kills the flat ones.

[IMAGE: an ape looking at a stopwatch with a confused expression, bananas in the other hand, leaning against a tree that is clearly not going to do anything]

the channel sounds different now

The other thing the ape fixed was how the calls sound.

The auto-cards on the channel used to read like a hospital chart. DEVELOPING confidence sixty fired at fifty-two thousand market cap, top holder one-nine-point-one percent, twenty holders, two-thirty-per-minute flow, h-one plus one-forty-seven percent, liquidity eighteen thousand, bucket B right-tail capture, take plus two-fifty stop minus twenty-five seventy-two-hour hold. The ape was reading these and thinking, who would talk like this.

Now they read like a friend yelling across the bar:

moonshot punt on a fresh $52k DEV with 19% top1, flow’s stacking. Aping in — +250 take, -25 stop, ~90min if it doesn’t move.

That’s the same information. It’s just said by an ape, not a printer.

a few small repairs in the back room

While the ape was in there, a few things got cleaned up that nobody asked about but everybody benefits from.

The bots were stepping on each other. There are two — one for the public channel, one for the private. They were sharing a name tag. Fixed. Each one is its own creature now.

The RPC endpoints — those are the seven phones the ape uses to call the chain — were getting permanently retired after a single bad hour. If one phone got rate-limited, the ape would hang up forever. Now each phone gets a five-minute time-out and then the ape tries again. The phones recover.

The ape’s notebook was filling up with old observations nobody would ever read again. Twenty-two days of dust. The notebook was almost a third of a gigabyte. Sweep happens hourly now — old observations get burned, the notebook stays light.

what the test says

Before the ape committed to any of this, the trailing rope and the new gates got run against fourteen days of trees the ape had observed. Not the calls, but the whole forest. Every tree the system saw, what it would have done, where it would have ended up.

Old shape: fired on lots of trees, average outcome plus thirteen percent.

New shape: fires on less than half as many trees, average outcome plus thirty-seven percent.

That’s the math. Fewer climbs, deeper bananas. The strategy stopped being “spray bananas at things and hope” and started being “wait for the tree, ride the rope, walk down green.”

And — this is the part that mattered most — the moonshot bucket alone went from a sim that said plus thirteen to one that says plus forty-two. Because the moonshot tail is so much longer than the scalp tail. One moonshot at +250 cashes a single fire that would have taken three or four scalps to match.

what the ape is still nervous about

The numbers are sim-numbers. The ape will believe them when they show up in real fires.

Wallet’s still at two dollars. The trades are still on paper, even though the buy-and-sell wiring is in place. One config flag and the next call card spawns a real trade. The ape is taking a breath before flipping that switch — wants to see ten or twenty cards under the new mechanics first, just to be sure the math is what the math says it is.

The settle loop is sixty seconds. When a tree falls in a single second, the ape catches it on the ground, not at the rope. The trailing helps — locked floors mean even a late catch is closer to where you wanted to exit — but a faster price feed would help more. That’s a project for a different day.

the day, in one paragraph

Eleven dead trees and a two dollar wallet got the ape’s attention. The ape changed the rules. New rule: don’t climb falling trees. Newer rule: a tree that already paid you out can’t take it back. Newest rule: ninety minutes or the tree wasn’t the tree. The cards on the channel sound like the ape now. The numbers in the notebook say the new rules work better than the old. The wallet still has two dollars. The work continues.
